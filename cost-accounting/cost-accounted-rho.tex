\begin{abstract}
The rho calculus, the formal core of the Rholang smart-contract language,
is a reflective higher-order process calculus in which channels are
the quoted codes of processes and processes may be recovered from
channels by dequotation.  Rholang's current cost-accounting mechanism
---~the \emph{phlogiston} (phlo) system~--- is specified as an
extension of the calculus with digital signatures and token-carrying
rewrite rules.  In this note we show that the entire cost-accounting
layer can be \emph{translated back} into the pure rho calculus via a
compositional encoding.  Signatures become channels, tokens become
messages on those channels, and signed processes must consume
``fuel'' before they can communicate.  Five mediator infrastructure
processes (splitters and joiners) handle the bookkeeping between
combined and decomposed token representations.

Because the rho calculus is the formal core of Rholang, this
translation doubles as a blueprint for rearchitecting phlogiston
accounting in Rholang~1.2 and beyond:  cost control is expressible
\emph{within} the language, eliminating the need for a privileged
runtime extension and opening the door to user-definable fee
structures, composable metering contracts, and formal verification
of the accounting layer itself.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The rho calculus~\cite{MeredithRadestock2005} is a variant of
Milner's $\pi$-calculus~\cite{Milner1999} distinguished by its
support for \emph{reflection}: processes can be turned into channels
(via the quoting operator~$\quot{\cdot}$) and channels can be turned
back into processes (via the dequoting operator~$\drop{\cdot}$).
This reflective structure makes the rho calculus especially
well-suited as the formal core of a smart-contract language, and
indeed it is the theoretical foundation of
\textbf{Rholang}~\cite{RholangSpec}, the native language of the
RChain / F1R3FLY platform.

In production, every Rholang deploy is gated by a cost-accounting
mechanism:  the \emph{phlogiston} (phlo) system.  A deploy is
signed by one or more digital signatures, each associated with a
\emph{token balance}; execution consumes phlo proportionally to the
resources used.  The current implementation treats cost accounting as
a runtime concern, external to the calculus itself.

This paper demonstrates that the cost-accounting extension can be
faithfully \emph{encoded} in the pure rho calculus.  Because the rho
calculus is the core of Rholang, the encoding immediately suggests a
rearchitecture of phlogiston accounting:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Cost control within the language.}  Rather than a
    privileged runtime extension, phlo accounting becomes a Rholang
    program, subject to the same formal semantics and verification
    tools as any other contract.
  \item \textbf{Composable metering.}  User-definable fee structures
    and composable metering contracts become possible, because the
    accounting layer is written in the same language as the
    contracts it meters.
  \item \textbf{Formal verification.}  The translation is
    compositional and can be verified using the standard
    bisimulation and full-abstraction proof techniques of the rho
    calculus, including the ongoing Lean~4 formalization effort.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:rho} reviews the syntax and semantics of the pure
rho calculus.  Section~\ref{sec:cost} presents the cost-accounted
extension.  Section~\ref{sec:translation} gives the compositional
translation.  Section~\ref{sec:verification} verifies each of the
five rewrite rules.  Section~\ref{sec:architecture} discusses
implications for the Rholang architecture.

\section{The Pure Rho Calculus}
\label{sec:rho}

We recall the syntax and operational semantics of the rho calculus,
presented here as a graph-structured lambda theory (GSLT).

\subsection{Syntax}

Processes and names are mutually defined:
%
\begin{align}
  P, Q &\;::=\; \nil
        \;\mid\; \recv{y}{x}{P}
        \;\mid\; \send{x}{Q}
        \;\mid\; P \Par Q
        \;\mid\; \drop{x}
  \label{eq:proc-syntax}
  \\[4pt]
  x, y &\;::=\; \quot{P}
  \label{eq:name-syntax}
\end{align}

\noindent
The intended readings are:
\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=2em]
  \item[$\nil$]
    The \emph{stopped process}: does nothing.
  \item[$\recv{y}{x}{P}$]
    The process that \emph{waits} for a message on channel~$x$,
    binds the received name to~$y$ in~$P$, and continues as the
    resulting instance of~$P$.
    (In Rholang: \texttt{for( y <- x ) \{ P \}}.)
  \item[$\send{x}{Q}$]
    \emph{Sends} the code of process~$Q$ on channel~$x$.
    (In Rholang: \texttt{x!( Q )}.)
  \item[$P \Par Q$]
    The \emph{parallel composition} of~$P$ and~$Q$.
  \item[$\drop{x}$]
    \emph{Dequotes} (reflects) channel~$x$ back into a running
    process.
  \item[$\quot{P}$]
    The \emph{quote} (code) of process~$P$, used as a channel name.
\end{description}

\subsection{Free Names}

The $\forc$-comprehension binds~$y$.  Free names are computed
recursively:
%
\begin{align*}
  \FN(\nil)           &= \emptyset \\
  \FN(\recv{y}{x}{P}) &= \bigl(\FN(P) \setminus \{y\}\bigr) \cup \{x\} \\
  \FN(\send{x}{Q})    &= \FN(Q) \cup \{x\} \\
  \FN(P \Par Q)       &= \FN(P) \cup \FN(Q) \\
  \FN(\drop{x})       &= \{x\}
\end{align*}

\subsection{Structural Equivalence}

Structural equivalence, written~$\Seq$, is the smallest
equivalence relation on processes that includes
$\alpha$-equivalence and makes $(P, \Par, \nil)$ a commutative
monoid:
%
\begin{align*}
  P \Par \nil    &\Seq P           & \text{(identity)}   \\
  P \Par Q       &\Seq Q \Par P    & \text{(commutativity)} \\
  (P \Par Q) \Par R &\Seq P \Par (Q \Par R) & \text{(associativity)}
\end{align*}

\subsection{Rewrite Rules (Operational Semantics)}

The single computational step is the \textsc{Comm} rule
(communication):
%
\begin{equation}
  \recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q}
  \;\red\;
  \subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y}
  \label{eq:comm}
\end{equation}
%
subject to the contextual closure rules:
%
\begin{gather}
  \frac{P \red P'}{P \Par Q \red P' \Par Q}
  \qquad\text{(\textsc{Par})}
  \label{eq:par-ctx}
  \\[6pt]
  \frac{P \Seq P' \qquad P' \red Q' \qquad Q' \Seq Q}{P \red Q}
  \qquad\text{(\textsc{Struct})}
  \label{eq:struct-ctx}
\end{gather}

\begin{remark}[Semantic substitution]
  The notation $\subst{P}{u}{v}$ denotes \emph{semantic}
  substitution.  It coincides with ordinary syntactic substitution
  in all cases except dequotation, where:
  \[
    \subst{(\drop{x})}{\quot{Q}}{y}
    \;=\;
    \begin{cases}
      Q & \text{if } y \Neq x, \\
      \drop{x} & \text{otherwise,}
    \end{cases}
  \]
  and $\Neq$ denotes name equality.
  See~\cite{MeredithRadestock2005} for full details.
\end{remark}

\section{The Cost-Accounted Extension}
\label{sec:cost}

We now present an extension of the rho calculus with digital
signatures and a token-based cost-accounting mechanism.  This
extension captures the essential structure of phlogiston accounting
in Rholang: processes carry signatures, tokens carry balances, and
communication is gated by token consumption.

\subsection{Extended Syntax}

\begin{definition}[Token and system syntax]
  \label{def:cost-syntax}
  \begin{align}
    T &\;::=\; () \;\mid\; \sig \;\mid\; T {:} T
    \label{eq:token-syntax} \\
    S &\;::=\; P^{\sig} \;\mid\; T \;\mid\; S \Par S
    \label{eq:system-syntax} \\
    \sig &\;::=\; () \;\mid\; \hashf(\langle\mathit{signature}\rangle) \;\mid\; \sig \amp \sig
    \label{eq:sig-syntax}
  \end{align}
  where $P^{\sig}$ denotes a process~$P$ signed by a digital
  signature~$\sig$.
\end{definition}

The token $\sig {:} T$ is read as ``the signature~$\sig$ holds
the remaining balance~$T$.''

\subsection{Cost-Accounted Rewrite Rules}

The five rewrite rules of the cost-accounted calculus are:

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Rule 1} (single signature, whole redex):
\begin{equation}
  (\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig}
  \Par \sig {:} T
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  (\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig} \Par T
  \label{eq:rule1}
\end{equation}

\noindent
\textbf{Rule 2} (compound signature, whole redex, split tokens):
\begin{equation}
  (\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}
  \Par \sig_1 {:} T_1 \Par \sig_2 {:} T_2
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  (\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}
  \Par T_1 \Par T_2
  \label{eq:rule2}
\end{equation}

\noindent
\textbf{Rule 3} (compound signature, whole redex, combined token):
\begin{equation}
  (\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}
  \Par (\sig_1 \amp \sig_2) {:} T
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  (\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T
  \label{eq:rule3}
\end{equation}

\noindent
\textbf{Rule 4} (compound signature, split processes, combined token):
\begin{equation}
  (\recv{y}{x}{P})^{\sig_1}
  \Par (\send{x}{Q})^{\sig_2}
  \Par (\sig_1 \amp \sig_2) {:} T
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  (\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T
  \label{eq:rule4}
\end{equation}

\noindent
\textbf{Rule 5} (compound signature, split processes, split tokens):
\begin{equation}
  (\recv{y}{x}{P})^{\sig_1}
  \Par (\send{x}{Q})^{\sig_2}
  \Par \sig_1 {:} T_1 \Par \sig_2 {:} T_2
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  (\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}
  \Par T_1 \Par T_2
  \label{eq:rule5}
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
  Rules~1--5 can be understood as variations on a single theme:
  a communication (the \textsc{Comm} rule of the pure calculus)
  that is \emph{gated} by the consumption of a token whose
  signature matches the signature(s) of the communicating
  processes.  The five rules differ in whether the redex is
  signed as a whole or its components are signed separately,
  and whether the token is combined or split across signatures.
\end{remark}

\section{The Translation}
\label{sec:translation}

The central observation is that the cost-accounting mechanism is a
\emph{fuel-acquisition protocol}: before a signed process can
communicate, it must consume a token (fuel) from the channel
associated with its signature.  This protocol is expressible
entirely within the pure rho calculus.

\subsection{Signatures to Names}

We define $\mathcal{N} : \sig \to \text{Names}$:
%
\begin{align}
  \Nsem{()}
    &= \quot{\nil}
    \label{eq:nsem-unit}
  \\
  \Nsem{\hashf(\sigma)}
    &= \quot{H_\sigma}
    \quad\text{for a canonical process~$H_\sigma$ encoding $\sigma$}
    \label{eq:nsem-hash}
  \\
  \Nsem{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}
    &= \quot{\bigl(\drop{\Nsem{\sig_1}} \Par \drop{\Nsem{\sig_2}}\bigr)}
    \label{eq:nsem-amp}
\end{align}
%
The compound-signature case~\eqref{eq:nsem-amp} exploits
reflection: the name for~$\sig_1 \amp \sig_2$ is the quote of
the parallel composition of the dequotations of the two
component names.  This ensures injectivity of the encoding.

\subsection{Tokens to Processes}

We define $\mathcal{T} : T \to \text{Processes}$:
%
\begin{align}
  \Tsem{()}      &= \nil
    \label{eq:tsem-unit}
  \\
  \Tsem{\sig : T} &= \send{\Nsem{\sig}}{\Tsem{T}}
    \label{eq:tsem-send}
\end{align}
%
A token $\sig {:} T$ becomes a \emph{message} on the channel
$\Nsem{\sig}$ carrying the code of the remaining token~$T$ as
its payload.

\subsection{Signed Processes}
\label{sec:signed-procs}

The key idea: a signed process must \emph{first consume fuel}
from its signature channel before it can perform its intended
communication.  The leftover token (the payload of the fuel
message) is released into the continuation via dequotation.

\subsubsection{Atomic Signatures}

\begin{align}
  \Psem{(\recv{y}{x}{P})^{\sig}}
    &= \recv{t}{\Nsem{\sig}}{\bigl(\recv{y}{x}{(\drop{t} \Par \Psem{P})}\bigr)}
    \label{eq:psem-recv-atomic}
  \\[4pt]
  \Psem{(\send{x}{Q})^{\sig}}
    &= \recv{t}{\Nsem{\sig}}{\bigl(\send{x}{\Psem{Q}} \Par \drop{t}\bigr)}
    \label{eq:psem-send-atomic}
  \\[4pt]
  \Psem{(\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig}}
    &= \recv{t}{\Nsem{\sig}}{
        \bigl(
          \recv{y}{x}{(\drop{t} \Par \Psem{P})}
          \Par \send{x}{\Psem{Q}}
        \bigr)
      }
    \label{eq:psem-redex-atomic}
\end{align}

\noindent
In each case, the outermost $\forc$ on~$\Nsem{\sig}$ acts as a
\emph{fuel gate}: the process blocks until a token is available.

\subsubsection{Compound Signatures}

For $\sig_1 \amp \sig_2$, the process must acquire fuel from
\emph{both} component channels:
%
\begin{align}
  \Psem{(\recv{y}{x}{P})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}}
    &= \recv{t_1}{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{
        \recv{t_2}{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{
          \recv{y}{x}{(\drop{t_1} \Par \drop{t_2} \Par \Psem{P})}
        }
      }
    \label{eq:psem-recv-compound}
  \\[4pt]
  \Psem{(\send{x}{Q})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}}
    &= \recv{t_1}{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{
        \recv{t_2}{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{
          \bigl(\send{x}{\Psem{Q}} \Par \drop{t_1} \Par \drop{t_2}\bigr)
        }
      }
    \label{eq:psem-send-compound}
  \\[4pt]
  \Psem{(\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}}
    &= \recv{t_1}{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{
        \recv{t_2}{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{
          \bigl(
            \recv{y}{x}{(\drop{t_1} \Par \drop{t_2} \Par \Psem{P})}
            \Par \send{x}{\Psem{Q}}
          \bigr)
        }
      }
    \label{eq:psem-redex-compound}
\end{align}

\subsection{System Translation}

The system translation $\mathcal{S}$ is defined compositionally:
%
\begin{align}
  \Ssem{P^{\sig}}       &= \Psem{P^{\sig}}
    \label{eq:ssem-signed}
  \\
  \Ssem{T}              &= \Tsem{T}
    \label{eq:ssem-token}
  \\
  \Ssem{S_1 \Par S_2}   &= \Ssem{S_1} \Par \Ssem{S_2}
    \label{eq:ssem-par}
\end{align}

\subsection{Infrastructure Processes}
\label{sec:infrastructure}

Rules~2--5 involve situations where the granularity of the token
(combined vs.\ split) does not match the granularity expected by
the signed process.  We introduce two families of
\emph{infrastructure processes} to mediate:

\begin{definition}[Splitter]
  \label{def:splitter}
  The splitter converts a combined token for~$\sig_1 \amp \sig_2$
  into separate tokens for~$\sig_1$ and~$\sig_2$:
  \[
    \mathrm{Split}(\sig_1, \sig_2)
    \;=\;
    \recv{t}{\Nsem{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}}{
      \bigl(
        \send{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{\nil}
        \Par
        \send{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{\drop{t}}
      \bigr)
    }
  \]
  (In the persistent variant, the outermost $\forc$ is replicated.)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Joiner]
  \label{def:joiner}
  The joiner converts separate tokens for~$\sig_1$ and~$\sig_2$
  into a combined token for~$\sig_1 \amp \sig_2$:
  \[
    \mathrm{Join}(\sig_1, \sig_2)
    \;=\;
    \recv{t_1}{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{
      \recv{t_2}{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{
        \send{\Nsem{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}}{(\drop{t_1} \Par \drop{t_2})}
      }
    }
  \]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Persistent infrastructure]
  In practice, splitters and joiners should be \emph{persistent}
  (replicated) processes.  The standard rho-calculus encoding of
  replication via self-referencing through reflection
  (cf.~\cite{MeredithRadestock2005}) applies directly.
\end{remark}

\section{Verification}
\label{sec:verification}

We verify that each of the five cost-accounted rewrite rules is
faithfully simulated by the translation.

\subsection{Rule 1: Single Signature, Whole Redex}

\noindent
\textbf{Source:}\quad
$(\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig} \Par \sig {:} T
\;\longrightarrow\;
(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig} \Par T$

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Translation:}
\[
  \underbrace{
    \recv{t}{\Nsem{\sig}}{
      \bigl(
        \recv{y}{x}{(\drop{t} \Par \Psem{P})}
        \Par \send{x}{\Psem{Q}}
      \bigr)
    }
  }_{\Psem{(\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig}}}
  \;\Par\;
  \underbrace{
    \send{\Nsem{\sig}}{\Tsem{T}}
  }_{\Tsem{\sig:T}}
\]

\noindent
\textbf{Step 1} --- fuel consumption on~$\Nsem{\sig}$,
binding $t \mapsto \quot{\Tsem{T}}$:
\[
  \recv{y}{x}{\bigl(\drop{(\quot{\Tsem{T}})} \Par \Psem{P}\bigr)}
  \Par \send{x}{\Psem{Q}}
  \;\Seq\;
  \recv{y}{x}{\bigl(\Tsem{T} \Par \Psem{P}\bigr)}
  \Par \send{x}{\Psem{Q}}
\]

\noindent
\textbf{Step 2} --- communication on~$x$,
binding $y \mapsto \quot{\Psem{Q}}$:
\[
  \Tsem{T} \Par \Psem{\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y}}
  \;=\;
  \Ssem{(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig} \Par T}
  \quad\checkmark
\]

\subsection{Rule 2: Compound Signature, Whole Redex, Split Tokens}

\noindent
\textbf{Source:}\quad
$(\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}
\Par \sig_1 {:} T_1 \Par \sig_2 {:} T_2
\;\longrightarrow\;
(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T_1 \Par T_2$

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Translation} (using~\eqref{eq:psem-redex-compound}):
\begin{multline*}
  \recv{t_1}{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{
    \recv{t_2}{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{
      \bigl(
        \recv{y}{x}{(\drop{t_1} \Par \drop{t_2} \Par \Psem{P})}
        \Par \send{x}{\Psem{Q}}
      \bigr)
    }
  }
  \\
  \Par\;
  \send{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{\Tsem{T_1}}
  \;\Par\;
  \send{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{\Tsem{T_2}}
\end{multline*}

\noindent
\textbf{Step 1} --- fuel from~$\Nsem{\sig_1}$:
$t_1 \mapsto \quot{\Tsem{T_1}}$.

\noindent
\textbf{Step 2} --- fuel from~$\Nsem{\sig_2}$:
$t_2 \mapsto \quot{\Tsem{T_2}}$.

\noindent
\textbf{Step 3} --- \textsc{Comm} on~$x$:
\[
  \Tsem{T_1} \Par \Tsem{T_2} \Par \Psem{\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y}}
  \;=\;
  \Ssem{(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T_1 \Par T_2}
  \quad\checkmark
\]

\subsection{Rule 3: Compound Signature, Whole Redex, Combined Token}

\noindent
\textbf{Source:}\quad
$(\recv{y}{x}{P} \Par \send{x}{Q})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}
\Par (\sig_1 \amp \sig_2) {:} T
\;\longrightarrow\;
(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T$

\medskip\noindent
The whole-redex encoding~\eqref{eq:psem-redex-compound} listens
on both $\Nsem{\sig_1}$ and $\Nsem{\sig_2}$, but the combined
token lives on $\Nsem{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}$.  The
$\mathrm{Split}(\sig_1, \sig_2)$ infrastructure process
(Definition~\ref{def:splitter}) mediates:
%
\begin{align*}
  &\mathrm{Split}(\sig_1, \sig_2) \Par \send{\Nsem{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}}{\Tsem{T}}
  \\
  &\qquad\red\quad
  \send{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{\nil} \Par \send{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{\Tsem{T}}
\end{align*}
%
Now both fuel channels are populated and the reduction proceeds
as in Rule~2, yielding
$\nil \Par \Tsem{T} \Par \Psem{\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y}}
\Seq \Ssem{(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T}$.
\quad$\checkmark$

\subsection{Rule 4: Split Processes, Combined Token}

\noindent
\textbf{Source:}\quad
$(\recv{y}{x}{P})^{\sig_1}
\Par (\send{x}{Q})^{\sig_2}
\Par (\sig_1 \amp \sig_2) {:} T
\;\longrightarrow\;
(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T$

\medskip\noindent
The translations of the separately signed processes listen on
$\Nsem{\sig_1}$ and $\Nsem{\sig_2}$ respectively
(equations~\eqref{eq:psem-recv-atomic}
and~\eqref{eq:psem-send-atomic}).  The token lives on
$\Nsem{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}$.  Again, the splitter mediates:
%
\[
  \mathrm{Split}(\sig_1, \sig_2) \Par \send{\Nsem{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2}}{\Tsem{T}}
  \;\red\;
  \send{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{\nil}
  \Par \send{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{\Tsem{T}}
\]
%
After fuel consumption, the remainder is
$\nil \Par \Tsem{T} \Par \Psem{\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y}}
\Seq \Ssem{(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T}$.
\quad$\checkmark$

\subsection{Rule 5: Split Processes, Split Tokens}

\noindent
\textbf{Source:}\quad
$(\recv{y}{x}{P})^{\sig_1}
\Par (\send{x}{Q})^{\sig_2}
\Par \sig_1 {:} T_1 \Par \sig_2 {:} T_2
\;\longrightarrow\;
(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T_1 \Par T_2$

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Translation:}
\begin{multline*}
  \recv{t_1}{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{
    \recv{y}{x}{(\drop{t_1} \Par \Psem{P})}
  }
  \;\Par\;
  \recv{t_2}{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{
    \bigl(\send{x}{\Psem{Q}} \Par \drop{t_2}\bigr)
  }
  \\
  \Par\;
  \send{\Nsem{\sig_1}}{\Tsem{T_1}}
  \;\Par\;
  \send{\Nsem{\sig_2}}{\Tsem{T_2}}
\end{multline*}

\noindent
\textbf{Step 1} --- both fuel channels fire:
\[
  \recv{y}{x}{(\Tsem{T_1} \Par \Psem{P})}
  \;\Par\;
  \send{x}{\Psem{Q}} \Par \Tsem{T_2}
\]

\noindent
\textbf{Step 2} --- \textsc{Comm} on~$x$:
\[
  \Tsem{T_1} \Par \Psem{\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y}} \Par \Tsem{T_2}
  \;=\;
  \Ssem{(\subst{P}{\quot{Q}}{y})^{\sig_1 \amp \sig_2} \Par T_1 \Par T_2}
  \quad\checkmark
\]

\section{Implications for Rholang Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

Because the rho calculus is the formal core of Rholang, the
translation developed above is not merely a theoretical exercise
--- it is a \emph{blueprint} for rearchitecting the phlogiston
cost-accounting layer in Rholang~1.2 and beyond.

\subsection{Cost Control as a Rholang Program}

In the current architecture, phlogiston accounting is
implemented as a privileged runtime extension: the Rholang
virtual machine intercepts communications, checks balances,
and debits tokens before allowing reduction to proceed.  The
translation shows that this entire mechanism can be
\emph{internalized} as a Rholang program.

Concretely:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Each deploy's \emph{signature} becomes a Rholang
    \emph{channel name}, generated by quoting a canonical
    process that encodes the signer's identity.
  \item Each \emph{token} (phlo balance) becomes a
    \emph{message} on the corresponding signature channel.
  \item Each \emph{signed process} is wrapped in a fuel-gate:
    a $\forc$-comprehension on its signature channel that
    must receive a token before the process can proceed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Composable Metering}

Because the accounting layer is now written in Rholang, it
becomes \emph{composable}.  Different metering strategies can
be implemented as different Rholang contracts:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Flat-rate metering:}  A single token per
    communication.
  \item \textbf{Proportional metering:}  Tokens whose
    continuation-payload encodes the remaining balance,
    debited proportionally to resource consumption.
  \item \textbf{Delegated metering:}  Splitters and joiners
    (Section~\ref{sec:infrastructure}) allow one entity to
    fund another's computation by transferring tokens across
    signature channels.
  \item \textbf{Market-based metering:}  Token prices can
    float based on demand for particular signature channels,
    implementing a fee market entirely within Rholang.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Formal Verification}

The compositionality of the translation means it can be
verified using standard techniques:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Bisimulation:}  The translation preserves
    barbed bisimulation up to the infrastructure processes.
  \item \textbf{Full abstraction:}  The ongoing Lean~4
    formalization of the rho calculus
    can be extended to cover the cost-accounted extension
    and its translation.
  \item \textbf{Security:}  The fuel-gate pattern ensures
    that no process can communicate without first acquiring
    a valid token, which is a capability-security property
    expressible and provable within the calculus.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implementation Path}

For the F1R3FLY platform, we envision the following implementation
path:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Phase 1:}  Implement the translation as a
    source-to-source transformation in the Rholang~1.2 compiler,
    replacing the current runtime cost-accounting hooks.
  \item \textbf{Phase 2:}  Deploy splitters, joiners, and
    persistent infrastructure processes as system-level
    Rholang contracts on each F1R3Node shard.
  \item \textbf{Phase 3:}  Expose the signature-channel and
    token APIs to user contracts, enabling composable and
    user-definable metering.
  \item \textbf{Phase 4:}  Extend the Lean~4 formalization
    to cover the cost-accounted extension and verify the
    translation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We have shown that the cost-accounted extension of the rho
calculus --- capturing the essential structure of Rholang's
phlogiston system --- can be faithfully translated back into
the pure rho calculus.  The translation is compositional and
rests on a single idea: \emph{signatures become channels,
tokens become messages, and signed processes must consume
fuel before they can communicate.}

For the F1R3FLY platform, this result is both a theoretical
contribution and a practical blueprint.  By internalizing
cost accounting within Rholang itself, we eliminate the need
for a privileged runtime extension, enable composable and
formally verifiable metering, and open the door to
user-definable fee structures --- all without leaving the
expressive universe of the rho calculus.
